01 · The Case Editorial
By Max Mahon · Staff Analyst

Bangkok Bank — the oldest of the Thai financial trio and, at a discount to its tangible book value, also the most boring — is trading at nine-point-nine percent dividend yield and six-point-nine times earnings. On the Niwes scale, it clears all five gates of the 5-5-5-5 test, scoring seventy-four of one hundred.

Yields nine-point-nine, covered twice over by cash.

The case is not that BBL will compound at fifteen percent. It will not. The case is that at fifty-five percent of book value, the market is quoting the franchise at half its liquidation value — and paying one to hold.

Cash flow from operations covers the dividend two-point-one times over. Payout ratio sits at forty-three percent. What is priced in: slower loan growth, margin compression, and the structural drag of a Thai economy growing at two-point-one percent.

The Niwes framework does not ask whether the stock will double. It asks whether, held for ten to twenty years, the dividends alone will have paid for the position. At a nine-point-nine yield, the answer is arithmetic.
